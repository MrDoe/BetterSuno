# Old English (Anglo-Saxon) Language Skill

## Overview

Comprehensive skill for working with Old English (Englisc) — the language of the Anglo-Saxons spoken in England from roughly 450–1150 AD. Covers reading, writing, and translating texts in Old English with attention to grammar, vocabulary, meter, and historical authenticity.

## When to Use

- Translating Modern English → Old English
- Translating Old English → Modern English
- Writing original Old English prose or poetry
- Analyzing Old English grammar (declensions, conjugations, syntax)
- Working with Anglo-Saxon poetic meter (alliterative verse)
- Reading or analyzing Old English texts (Beowulf, Anglo-Saxon Chronicle, riddles)
- Generating Old English names, inscriptions, or short passages
- Explaining Old English to learners

## Old English Quick Reference

### Alphabet and Orthography

Old English was written with insular script letter forms. In modern editions, the following special characters appear:

| Character | Name | IPA | Notes |
|-----------|------|-----|-------|
| **æ** (ash) | æsc | /æ/ | As in Modern English "cat" |
| **þ** (thorn) | þorn | /θ/ or /ð/ | Voiceless or voiced "th" |
| **ð** (eth) | eð | /θ/ or /ð/ | Interchangeable with þ in many manuscripts |
| **ƿ** (wynn) | ƿynn | /w/ | Often modernized to "w" |
| **ʒ** (yogh) | yoʒ | /g/, /j/, /ɣ/ | Modernized to "g" |

Modern convention: use **þ** and **ð** for th-sounds, **æ** for ash, **w** for wynn.

### Grammar Overview

Old English is an inflected language, similar to Latin or modern German. Nouns, adjectives, pronouns, and determiners decline for case, number, and gender.

#### Cases
- **Nominative**: subject
- **Accusative**: direct object
- **Genitive**: possession
- **Dative**: indirect object, instrument
- **Instrumental**: means (vestigial, merged with dative)

#### Grammatical Gender
- Masculine (se)
- Feminine (sēo)
- Neuter (þæt)

#### Noun Declensional Classes
Nouns fall into strong and weak declensions with sub-classes (a-stem, ō-stem, n-stem, etc.):

**Strong Masculine a-stem (stān — "stone"):**

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nom. | stān | stānas |
| Acc. | stān | stānas |
| Gen. | stānes | stāna |
| Dat. | stāne | stānum |

**Strong Feminine ō-stem (giefu — "gift"):**

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nom. | giefu | giefa |
| Acc. | giefe | giefa |
| Gen. | giefe | giefa |
| Dat. | giefe | giefum |

#### Verb Conjugation

**Strong verbs** (ablaut/vowel gradation) — 7 classes, distinguished by vowel patterns in the four principal parts: infinitive, past singular, past plural, past participle.

*Example: Class I (wrītan — "to write")*

| Form | Old English |
|------|-------------|
| Infinitive | wrītan |
| Past 1st/3rd sg. | wrāt |
| Past 2nd sg. | write |
| Past plural | writon |
| Past participle | writen |

**Weak verbs** (dental suffix) — 3 classes. Form past tense with -d/-t/-ð suffix.

*Example: Class 1 (hīeran — "to hear")*

| Form | Old English |
|------|-------------|
| Infinitive | hīeran |
| Past 1st/3rd sg. | hīerde |
| Past plural | hīerdon |
| Past participle | hīered |

#### Present Tense Conjugation (hīeran — "to hear")

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | iċ hīere | wē hīeraþ |
| 2nd | þū hīerst | gē hīeraþ |
| 3rd | hē hīerþ | hīe hīeraþ |

#### Preterite-Present Verbs

These verbs have present forms derived from original past tense forms (similar to Modern English modal verbs):

- **cunnan** (can, know how to)
- **magan** (may, be able to)
- **sculan** (shall, must)
- **willan** (will, want to)
- **witan** (to know)
- **āgan** (to own, possess)

#### Word Order

Old English word order is more flexible than Modern English but follows patterns:

- **Main clauses**: typically Subject-Verb-Object (SVO), but Verb-Subject (VS) common after adverbs like *þā* (then).
- **Subordinate clauses**: typically Subject-Object-Verb (SOV) or verb-final.
- **Questions**: Verb-Subject inversion, often with the particle *hū* (how), *hwæt* (what), *hwā* (who).

## Translation Guidelines

### Modern English → Old English

1. **Identify part of speech** for each word to determine correct declension/conjugation.
2. **Determine case** for nouns and adjectives based on grammatical role.
3. **Select appropriate register** — poetic (elevated, kennings, compound words) or prose (simpler vocabulary).
4. **Respect default word order** — SOV in subordinate clauses, SVO or VS in main clauses.
5. **Use historical vocabulary** — avoid anachronisms. When a concept didn't exist in Anglo-Saxon England, use a kenning (descriptive compound) or a loan adaptation with explanation.

**Common pitfalls:**
- Modern English "dream" ≠ OE *drēam* (joy, mirth). Use *mǣting* or *swefn* for dream.
- Modern English "bread" ≠ OE *brēad* (crumb, bit). Use *hlāf* for bread.
- Modern English "deer" ≠ OE *dēor* (wild animal, beast). Use *heorot* for deer.
- The verb "to be" has TWO infinitives: *bēon* (permanent/future) and *wesan* (temporary/present), though often used interchangeably.

### Old English → Modern English

1. **Identify inflected forms** — look at endings to determine case, number, person.
2. **Locate the main verb** — it may be at the end of the clause (especially in subordinate clauses).
3. **Resolve compound words and kennings** — OE poetry uses elaborate descriptive compounds (*swan-rād* "swan-road" = sea, *bān-hūs* "bone-house" = body).
4. **Watch for poetic syntax** — hyperbaton (displaced words), variation (repetition with different words describing same thing), and litotes (understatement) are common.
5. **Consult a dictionary** — Bosworth-Toller is the standard reference. See Resources section below.

### Poetic Meter (Alliterative Verse)

Old English poetry uses stress-based alliterative meter. Each line is divided into two half-lines (a-verse and b-verse) linked by alliteration:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Half-lines** | Each line has two half-lines separated by a caesura |
| **Stresses** | Each half-line has two stressed syllables (lifts) |
| **Alliteration** | The first lift of the b-verse must alliterate with one or both lifts of the a-verse |
| **Anacrusis** | Optional unstressed syllables before the first lift |
| **Dips** | Unstressed syllables between lifts |

**Example (Beowulf, opening lines):**

> Hwæt! Wē Gār-Dena in ġēar-dagum
> þēod-cyninga þrym ġefrūnon,
> hū ðā æþelingas ellen fremedon!

*Translation:* So! We have heard of the Spear-Danes' glory in days of yore, of the people's kings, how those nobles performed deeds of valor!

*Alliteration pattern (bold):* **Ġ**ār-dena / **ġ**ēar-dagum; **þ**ēod-cyninga / **þ**rym; **æ**þelingas / **e**llen (all vowels alliterate with each other!).

## Translation Workflow

### Phase 1: Analysis & Preparation

1. Identify the source language and text type (prose/poetry/inscription).
2. If translating into OE, note anachronisms and prepare kenning substitutions.
3. Gather reference materials (dictionary, grammar guide for target register).

### Phase 2: Grammatical Mapping

1. Parse sentences — identify main verb, subject, objects, subordinate clauses.
2. For OE output: determine case assignments, select appropriate noun/adj declension class.
3. For OE input: identify inflected forms, resolve ambiguous endings.

### Phase 3: Translation

1. Produce literal translation first — get the grammar and vocabulary right.
2. For OE output: adjust word order to OE norms (SOV in subordinate clauses, VS after adverbs).
3. For poetic output: compose with alliterative meter, kennings, and variation.
4. Provide both the Old English text and a Modern English gloss.

### Phase 4: Verification

1. Verify all inflected forms against reference grammars.
2. Check that kennings and compounds are historically plausible.
3. For poetry: scan each line for correct alliteration and stress patterns.
4. Note any uncertainties or alternative readings.

## Examples

### Example 1: The Lord's Prayer (ModE → OE)

**Modern English:**
Our Father, who art in heaven, hallowed be thy name. Thy kingdom come, thy will be done, on earth as it is in heaven.

**Old English (West Saxon, ca. 1000 AD):**
Fæder ūre, þū þe eart on heofonum, sī þīn nama ġehālgod. Tōbecume þīn rīċe. Ġeweorðe þīn willa on eorðan swā swā on heofonum.

**Literal gloss:**
Father our, you who are in heavens, be your name hallowed. May-come your kingdom. May-be-done your will on earth just-as in heavens.

### Example 2: Short Inscription (ModE → OE)

**Request:** "I am a warrior of the North. My sword is sharp, my shield is strong."

**Old English:**
Iċ eom gūþ-wiġa norðanweardes. Mīn sweord is scearp, mīn sċyld is strang.

**Literal gloss:**
I am war-fighter of-the-north. My sword is sharp, my shield is strong.

### Example 3: OE Riddle Translation

**Old English (Exeter Book Riddle 47):**
Moððe word fræt. Mē þæt þūhte
wrǣtliċu wyrd, þā iċ þæt wundor ġefræġn,
þæt se wyrm forswealg wera ġied sumes,
þēof in þȳstro, þrymfæstne cwide
ond þæs strangan staþol.

**Modern English translation:**
A moth ate words. That seemed to me
a wondrous fate, when I learned of that marvel,
that the worm swallowed the speech of a certain man,
a thief in darkness, the glorious utterance
and its strong foundation.

## Limitations
- This skill provides linguistic patterns and reference material. It is not a substitute for scholarly expertise in Old English philology.
- Translations produced are approximations; Old English dialectal variation (West Saxon, Northumbrian, Mercian, Kentish) means there is rarely a single "correct" form.
- Poetic composition in alliterative meter requires significant practice — initial output may have metrical imperfections.
- Stop and clarify if the required dialect, time period (Early vs. Late Old English), or register (poetic vs. prose) is not specified.
